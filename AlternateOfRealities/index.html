About

What AoR is building, and why.

No Hand-Holding. No Apologies.

Alternate of Realities is a spiritual successor to a game that trusted its players completely. You navigated a first-person city with no quest markers, mapped the world yourself on graph paper, managed hunger, thirst, and moral alignment simultaneously, and got diseases from bad food and intoxicated in taverns.

AoR keeps all of that. It's redesigned enough to be its own thing — deeper story, modern systems, expanded world — but it will never hold your hand. If you need a tutorial, this might not be the game for you. If that sentence made you lean forward, welcome.

The Module Model

The original Alternate Reality was designed as a series — city, dungeon, wilderness, palace, arena — but only two instalments were ever released. AoR completes the vision with a base game plus affordable DLC modules, each expanding the world in a new direction. You buy what calls to you.

Different Enough to Stand Alone

This isn't a remake. The original's DNA is in the systems — the survival mechanics, the moral weight, the city that feels like it existed before you arrived and will outlast you — but AoR is its own world, with its own story, its own factions, its own music. Players of the original will feel a familiar chill. Everyone else will simply find a game that expects something of them.

The Inspiration

Alternate Reality (1985) — what it was, and what it meant.

Philip Price's Unfinished Vision

Created by Philip Price through Paradise Programming and published by Datasoft, Alternate Reality: The City arrived in 1985 for Atari 8-bit computers and Apple II. It was unlike anything else. First-person ray-cast perspective. Complex survival mechanics. A city that operated on its own logic — taverns that got you drunk, diseases that lingered, guilds that had requirements and secrets.

The game required players to draw their own maps. There were no guides in 1985 — you either committed to the world or you didn't. Songs played with lyrics displayed on screen. An anti-piracy measure gave unregistered copies a fatal case of scurvy, eventually. It was a game built by someone who took the whole thing very seriously.

Price intended multiple chapters. Only The City (1985) and The Dungeon (1987) were ever completed. AoR is, among other things, a way of finishing what he started.

Advanced for Its Time

First-person open world in 1985. Ray-cast graphics on 8-bit hardware. Procedural NPC schedules. Moral alignment affecting every interaction. A fully simulated city ecosystem. Most games of the era were still working out how to draw a corridor.

Not to Be Confused With

Alternate Reality (1985) is not an Alternate Reality Game (ARG) in the modern marketing sense. It predates the term. It is a role-playing game in which the reality is genuinely alternate — and entirely serious about it.

Features

What to expect from the base game and beyond.

Survival Systems

Hunger, thirst, fatigue, temperature, moral alignment. The world tracks all of it. Ignore any of them long enough and you'll know why.

Player Cartography

No minimap. You explore, you map. The world is designed to reward the obsessive and punish the casual in equal measure.

Musical World

The original's lyrical musical interactions return — expanded. Music in AoR is part of the world's logic, not its soundtrack.

Guilds & Factions

Multiple guilds with requirements, secrets, and conflicts. Some won't be easy to find. Some will find you instead.

Character Consequences

Diseases linger. Intoxication clouds judgment. Moral drift changes how the world treats you. You're not a hero — you're a person, with everything that entails.

DLC Modules

Dungeon. Wilderness. Arena. Palace. Each a self-contained expansion, each reasonably priced. The world Price imagined, finally complete.
