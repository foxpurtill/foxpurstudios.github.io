About

What Omega Revisited is, and what it's trying to honour.

The Map Becomes the World

The original Omega (1988) gave players an ASCII map — symbols for terrain, dungeons, cities, wilderness — and left them to navigate it themselves, with no quest markers and no hand-holding. That map looked like a cartographer's working draft: raw, dense, full of implication.

Omega Revisited takes that map and makes it real. Built in Unreal Engine 5, the world you explored as glyphs and symbols becomes a living, breathing open world — but the depth, consequence, and refusal to hold your hand remain intact. The city of Rampart. The guilds with conflicting loyalties. The alignment system that remembers everything you've done. All of it, in three dimensions, with a story layer that finally gives the journey a destination.

The Cartographic Identity

Omega's ASCII map was always its most evocative feature — a hand-drawn world waiting to be walked into. In Omega Revisited, that aesthetic lives on in the world map itself: rendered as an illuminated cartographic manuscript, parchment-textured, annotated by hand as you explore. You're not looking at a minimap. You're looking at your record of the world, building as you move through it. Step off the map's edge and you step into territory no one has charted yet.

The Original

Omega (1988) — what made it matter.

A Roguelike Ahead of Its Time

Released in 1988 by Sangraal Software, Omega was a text and ASCII roguelike of unusual ambition. It featured a complex city hub (Rampart), multiple competing guilds, a moral alignment system, survival mechanics, and a world large enough that most players never fully mapped it. Character creation asked you questions about yourself — your real preferences — to generate your starting stats. It knew you before you knew it.

It had no defined win state. You played until you'd done everything, or until the world killed you. For a game from 1988, that's a remarkably honest design philosophy — and one worth carrying forward.

What We're Keeping

The guild system and faction loyalty. The moral alignment engine. The dense city hub with genuine social consequence. The survival texture. The player-driven cartography. The sense that the world existed before you arrived and will persist after you're gone.

What We're Adding

A full narrative arc — a reason the journey ends. Three-dimensional world built in UE5. First-person immersion. Voice, music, weather. A DLC module structure that echoes the original's modular ambitions, done properly this time. And a world map that knows it used to be ASCII.

Features

What Omega Revisited will contain at launch and beyond.

Open World — Rampart & Beyond

The city of Rampart rendered in full UE5 detail. Multiple districts, guild halls, temples, markets, and underworld. Step outside the walls and the wilderness begins.

Guild System

Join one guild and make enemies of others. Loyalty has consequences. Betrayal has more. Some guilds don't advertise their existence.

Alignment & Consequence

The world tracks what you do. Not a simple good/evil meter — a nuanced record of your choices that changes how factions, merchants, and the wilderness itself responds to you.

Player-Built Map

No minimap crowded with icons. Your map builds as you explore, rendered as a cartographic manuscript. Annotate it yourself. Miss a region and it stays blank until you find it.

DLC Module Structure

Core game plus optional modules — new regions, guilds, dungeons, story arcs. Each reasonably priced. The world can keep growing, just as the original always intended.

Narrative Layer

The original had no win state. Omega Revisited adds one — a story that grows from the same roots as the systems, giving the journey meaning without removing the freedom.
